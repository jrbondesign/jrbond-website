Executive Summary

Pemly is Slack for home-service teams, powered by AI. Every job gets its own channel where the crew, the office, and an AI assistant stay in sync. A delayed liner delivery doesn't turn into an angry customer call three days later.

I know this problem personally. Some of my closest friends work in the trades, and I've watched this play out on their jobs for years. The job isn't what breaks. The communication is. Updates get buried in personal texts. Photos scatter across six phones. The owner spends evenings stitching the day back together. I built Pemly to fix that, entirely in code with Claude Code. It's live in private beta now.

The Real Problem

Background: Home-service teams run their jobs through texts, voicemails, and group chats. The truth about any job lives in six places, and the office usually hears about a problem from the angry customer, not the crew.

The catch: These are people working from a truck, not a desk. Anything that requires training or behavior change is dead on arrival. The bar wasn't "better than Slack." It was "as easy as the group text they already use, but organized around the job."

Designing Around the Job, Not the Topic

One channel per job. Channels aren't organized by topic like Slack. Every channel is a job. Crew, office, customer details, photos, and history all live where the work lives. When a callback shows up eight months later, the whole job history is one search away.

The crew posts like they text. If you can send a text, you can use Pemly on day one. Updates, photos, and questions go up in seconds from any phone. No training, no manuals. That's the adoption strategy.

AI that moves work forward. Pemly Assistant reads what's happening on each job and does the busywork that usually lands on the owner at night. It summarizes the day, flags delays before they blow up, and drafts the customer update for the office to approve. It's not a chatbot bolted on. It's a teammate in the channel.

The human/AI handoff. The hardest design problem was trust: what should the AI do on its own, and what needs a human? Drafting a customer update is safe to automate. Sending it is not. Getting that line right shaped the whole assistant experience.

Built in Code, With AI

No Figma. No mockups. No handoff. I designed every interaction by building the real thing and using it. And since the product's core feature is an AI assistant, there was a compounding effect: I was designing AI interactions while using AI to design. Each side sharpened the other.

The result is a working product with live auth, real job channels, and role-based views for owners, office, project managers, and crew. The AI assistant experience is fully designed and prototyped in the product while I build out the backend. All of it by one person.

What This Proves

It's live, not a concept. Private beta at pemly.app with working auth, job channels, and role-based views for every member of a home-service team. The assistant is prototyped in the product today.

I know the user. Close friends work in the trades and live this problem every day, so I hear about it firsthand.

New interaction patterns, from scratch. The assistant required inventing patterns for AI trust, approval, and handoff. There was nothing to copy.

This is how I work now. Designed for the field. Built in code. Shipped solo. AI at the core of the product and the process.
